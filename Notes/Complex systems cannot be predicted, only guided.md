When dealing with complex systems - whether organizations, software architectures, or creative processes - attempting to predict exact outcomes and control every variable leads to brittleness and failure.

Instead, effective intervention works through:

1. **Setting clear constraints and principles** rather than prescribing solutions
2. **Creating feedback loops** that allow the system to self-correct
3. **Observing patterns** rather than trying to control individual elements

This idea emerged from multiple sources:

Kevin Kelly's [[Out of Control]] describes how the most robust systems in nature and technology share this quality. Ecosystems, markets, and neural networks all work through emergence rather than central planning.

[[Sarah Chen]]'s approach to product development at [[TechCorp]] embodies this: her team defines principles (fast load times, accessible to screen readers, mobile-first) but doesn't prescribe exact implementations. Solutions emerge from these constraints.

The same principle applies to [[Design systems]] - the best ones create boundaries that guide without constraining. They're frameworks for possibility, not templates for replication.
